# Analysis Frameworks — the lenses Campaign Atlas uses

This file gives the skills a consistent strategic vocabulary. Read it before producing any deep-dive or transplant.

## The core lens: Insight → Tension → Idea → Mechanic → Execution → Effect

Every great campaign can be read through this six-step chain. Skills follow it as the spine of analysis.

| Step | What it is | What it is NOT |
|---|---|---|
| **Insight** | A specific, observable truth about how people behave or feel — usually surprising or under-said | A platitude ("women want to feel beautiful"), a demographic stat, or a brand value |
| **Tension** | The cultural, category, or product tension the brand can resolve or weaponize | A trend report. Tension is sharper than trend. |
| **Idea** | The strategic bet — one sentence anyone in the room can repeat | The tagline. The tagline is downstream of the idea. |
| **Mechanic** | The reusable structural move — what the campaign DOES, not what it looks like | The execution. Wrapped's mechanic is "first-party data → identity asset"; the execution is the slides. |
| **Execution** | The crafted output — film, OOH, app, stunt, product change | A media plan |
| **Effect** | What measurably changed — sales, share, brand metrics, behavior, culture | Impressions or views absent any business outcome |

When deep-diving, name each step explicitly. When transplanting, you transfer the **mechanic** — not the execution.

## Effectiveness benchmarks

Use these public benchmarks when evaluating claimed effects.

### The Binet & Field 60/40 rule
- **60% of investment** should go to long-term brand building (broad reach, emotional, low-frequency)
- **40% to short-term sales activation** (targeted, rational, high-frequency)
- Below 60/40 → brand erodes. Above 70/30 → activation efficiency drops. Source: IPA Databank, *The Long and the Short of It*, 2013.

### The 5 effectiveness multipliers (Binet & Field, *Effectiveness in Context*)
Campaigns score higher on effectiveness when they:
1. Reach all category buyers (not just current customers)
2. Sustain across years (not one-quarter blasts)
3. Are emotionally distinctive
4. Build "fame" — talked-about beyond the audience
5. Use multi-channel mixes

### How Brands Grow (Sharp / Ehrenberg-Bass)
- **Physical availability** + **mental availability** = brand growth
- Penetration matters more than loyalty
- Light buyers drive most of category sales
- Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs) = colors, characters, jingles, logos that act as memory shortcuts

### Attention
Karen Nelson-Field's research: **attention seconds**, not impressions, predict business outcomes. A 6-sec ad with 2 attention seconds underperforms a 6-sec ad with 5.

## Strategic frames you'll see in award case studies

Use these as labels in deep-dives and transplants.

### Mechanic types (non-exhaustive)

- **Product-as-message** — modify the product itself to deliver the message (AXA Three Words, REI #OptOutside closing on Black Friday, Whopper Detour using competitor stores as activation)
- **Data-as-creative** — convert first-party data into a personalized cultural moment (Spotify Wrapped, Apple "Year in Review", Strava Year in Sport)
- **Co-opt the enemy's codes** — adopt the visual / linguistic codes of a category you're disrupting (Liquid Death using metal/energy aesthetics for water; Oatly's anti-marketing copy)
- **Ritual creation** — manufacture a time-bound recurring moment users come to expect (Wrapped, Black Friday, McDonald's McRib return)
- **Cultural collision** — collide your brand with a non-obvious cultural movement (Burger King × scary clowns at Halloween, Kraft Mac & Cheese × Salt Bae)
- **Earned-first** — design the work to generate news/creator/PR coverage as the primary distribution (Aviation Gin Peloton response, Burger King Moldy Whopper)
- **Hack the medium** — exploit a platform or format rule no one else noticed (Snickers "You're Not You When You're Hungry" word-typo SEO targeting; Spotify Spreadbeats ads as 8-bit visualizers in song play; Heinz "Draw Ketchup")
- **Service-as-marketing** — the campaign IS a free service users want (Volvo LifePaint, Nike Run Club, Patagonia repair tour)
- **Long-term brand asset construction** — build a Distinctive Brand Asset that compounds across years (Geico Gecko, Compare the Market meerkats, Old Spice "the man your man could smell like" continuity)
- **Stunt with depth** — a one-off stunt anchored to a real product or service truth (KFC FCK apology page, Patagonia "Don't Buy This Jacket", IKEA pee stick)
- **Creator-led co-ownership** — give creators creative control + economic stake, not just a sponsorship fee (Mr Beast × Feastables, Glossier early-influencer model)

### Brand archetypes (Mark & Pearson)

Campaigns often play to one of 12 archetypes. Useful shorthand for fit:

Hero · Outlaw · Magician · Sage · Jester · Lover · Innocent · Explorer · Caregiver · Creator · Ruler · Everyperson.

A scrappy challenger leaning Outlaw (Liquid Death, Oatly, Brewdog) shouldn't be transplanted to a brand whose archetype is Caregiver (Dove, Johnson & Johnson) without translation.

### Behavioral / cognitive levers

Useful when explaining "why it works":

- **Social proof** (Cialdini)
- **Loss aversion** (Kahneman)
- **Identity signaling** (Berger, *Contagious*)
- **Hyperbolic discounting** (BJ Fogg, *Tiny Habits*)
- **Costly signaling** (evolutionary economics — applies to brand premium)
- **The endowment effect** (free-trial mechanics)
- **The peak-end rule** (experience marketing)
- **Scarcity & FOMO** (limited drops, members-only)
- **Reciprocity** (give-first marketing — free tools, useful content)

### Channel architectures

When describing a campaign's channel logic:

- **Lead asset** — the single piece that anchors the campaign (film, OOH, product, microsite, tool)
- **Amplifiers** — supporting channels that extend the lead
- **Earned hook** — what makes press/creators/users talk about it for free
- **Always-on layer** — the social/SEO/CRM bed underneath the campaign moment
- **Retargeting layer** — what catches the people the lead asset moved

A great campaign has an earned hook by design — not as an afterthought.

## The "scam ad" test

A campaign is suspect if any of:
- No paid media spend on the work, but big award entries
- Brand has never publicly mentioned it (no newsroom, no social, no investor mention)
- The agency case study is the only source
- Trade press has flagged it (AdAge, Campaign, Storyboard18 investigations)
- Awards body has opened investigation or rescinded

Skills must surface this flag, not silently include the work.
